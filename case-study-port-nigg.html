Port Nigg Energy Recovery Facility: originating a 25MW private-wire EfW project in an industrial freeport

A project development paper documenting the commercial structuring, technical design, regulatory positioning, and stakeholder engagement undertaken by the FTP development team in connection with a proposed ERF at the Port of Nigg, Cromarty Firth.

Arjit Gupta  &  Allan Whitelaw
Arjit Gupta — Director, First Tactical Projects Ltd  ·  Principal, Elementa Advisory Ltd
Allan Whitelaw — Director, First Tactical Projects Ltd
With contributions from the FTP development team
Location Port Nigg, Cromarty Firth, Scotland
Project scale 25MW / est. £250–300M CAPEX
Sector Energy Recovery / Waste-to-Energy
Freeport zone Inverness & Cromarty Firth Green Freeport
Stage reached Advanced pre-FEED commercial structuring

Context and strategic rationale

Port Nigg occupies a strategically significant position within the Inverness and Cromarty Firth Green Freeport — one of the first two Scottish freeports designated under the UK Government's freeport programme. As a candidate hub for FLOW offshore wind component manufacturing and energy-intensive industrial activity, its underlying power constraint is acute: existing grid capacity of approximately 8–10MW is insufficient to support large-scale tenant demand, and SSEN reinforcement to a confirmed 30MW connection is not expected until later part of this decade at the earliest.

FTP identified this constraint as both a development problem and a project opportunity. A dispatchable, privately-financed Energy Recovery Facility — delivering power via private wire before grid reinforcement arrives — offered a credible solution that required no public subsidy and carried zero capital risk to the site owner.

The strategic case was simple: the port needs cost-competitive, non-intermittent power by 2030. An ERF operating on a Design-Build-Finance-Transfer model could deliver 25MW of baseload capacity on commercial terms, structured so that all development and financing risk sits with the developer, not the site.

Technology selection

FTP conducted a structured evaluation of energy technologies capable of meeting the port's needs: onshore wind, solar PV, SMR nuclear, diesel generation, hydrogen, and moving-grate ERF with combined heat and power. The evaluation tested each against six criteria — dispatchability, technology readiness, delivery timeline, financing tractability, planning risk, and private-wire compatibility.

ERF was selected as the preferred technology on the basis of firm, programmable output (unlike wind and solar), proven UK and European deployment track record, a delivery timeline of 4–6 years from financial close, strong bankability under project finance structures, and a feedstock economic model that generates dual revenue — gate fees from waste acceptance and electricity revenue from the PPA — materially improving debt service coverage.

Commercial structure

The project was structured as a Design-Build-Finance-(Operate)-Transfer arrangement. FTP bears all development, permitting, EPC, and financing risk. A 20-year take-or-pay Power Purchase Agreement at terms competitive to the retail power price forms the contractual spine of the financing model, targeting senior debt at approximately 70% gearing. Gate fees from waste intake — modelled at a blended average of approximately £108/tonne across 300–350ktpa — provide the second major revenue stream and function as a significant natural hedge to power price volatility.

The Freeport private-wire model provides material cost advantages: exemption from transmission and distribution use-of-system charges (TNUoS and DUoS), exemption from balancing services charges (BSUoS), and Ofgem licensing simplification. These translate directly into a lower delivered cost per unit compared with grid-imported alternatives at the current connection cost trajectory.

Feedstock strategy

A three-stream feedstock model was developed targeting an aggregate of 300–350ktpa. Primary supply was identified from Highland Council municipal solid waste residual streams, with secondary supply from commercial and industrial waste via the Longman transfer station and equivalent facilities across the H&I region. Grade B and C waste wood from construction activity and forestry processing in the Highlands formed a third stream, aligned with Scottish Government residual waste policy and the requirement for R1 energy recovery classification under the Industrial Emissions Directive.

Regulatory and permitting pathway

The permitting architecture was mapped across four concurrent tracks: SEPA integrated environmental permit under the IED, Highland Council planning permission (with EIA scope), Marine Scotland marine licence for barge operations, and Ofgem private-wire notification. A Carbon Capture and Storage integration route was identified as a future value-accretive pathway aligned with the North Sea Transition Authority's interest in CCS-ready industrial installations in the Cromarty Firth corridor. Exploratory meetings with The Highlands Council confirmed the joint appetite to support such a project.

Outcome and reflection

The project did not proceed to the next development stage. The site owner confirmed that decommissioning of the Nigg Oil Terminal — the intended ERF location — had overrun its contractual completion date, making near-term site exclusivity unavailable. Alongside this, uncertainty about the tenant demand mix at the port during the critical 2026–2029 window made the 25MW take-or-pay structure difficult for the counterparty to commit to. These were exogenous timing factors, not failures of the development proposition itself.

In infrastructure origination, the discipline of building a bankable proposition — stress-testing technology assumptions, financing structures, and regulatory pathways against real counterparty engagement — creates durable institutional knowledge. The Port Nigg programme established FTP's capability across the full development lifecycle of an ERF project, from concept to commercial-ready proposition, in a live market environment.

The work also produced several transferable insights: the critical role of site exclusivity timing in private-wire ERF financing; the structural mismatch between long-term take-or-pay tenors and the planning horizons of industrial port operators managing evolving tenant mixes; and the material financing advantage of dual-revenue-stream projects in improving debt service coverage and reducing PPA pricing pressure.
