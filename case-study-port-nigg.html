Port Nigg Energy Recovery Facility: originating a 25MW private-wire EfW project in an industrial freeport

A project development paper documenting the commercial structuring, technical design, regulatory positioning, and stakeholder engagement undertaken by the FTP development team in connection with a proposed ERF at the Port of Nigg, Cromarty Firth.

01 — Strategic Context

A critical power constraint as both a development problem and a project opportunity

Port Nigg occupies a strategically significant position within the Inverness and Cromarty Firth Green Freeport — one of the first two Scottish freeports designated under the UK Government's freeport programme. As a candidate hub for FLOW offshore wind component manufacturing and energy-intensive industrial activity, its underlying power constraint is acute: existing grid capacity of approximately 8–10MW is insufficient to support large-scale tenant demand, and SSEN reinforcement to a confirmed 30MW connection is not expected until later this decade at the earliest.

FTP identified this constraint as both a development problem and a project opportunity. A dispatchable, privately-financed Energy Recovery Facility — delivering power via private wire before grid reinforcement arrives — offered a credible solution that required no public subsidy and carried zero capital risk to the site owner.

The strategic case was clear: the port needs cost-competitive, non-intermittent power by 2030. An ERF operating on a Design-Build-Finance-Transfer model could deliver 25MW of baseload capacity on commercial terms, with all development and financing risk sitting with the developer, not the site.

02 — Technology Selection

A structured technology evaluation across six criteria yielded a clear preferred solution

FTP conducted a structured evaluation of energy technologies capable of meeting the port's needs: onshore wind, solar PV, SMR nuclear, diesel generation, hydrogen, and moving-grate ERF with combined heat and power. The evaluation tested each against six criteria — dispatchability, technology readiness, delivery timeline, financing tractability, planning risk, and private-wire compatibility.

ERF was selected as the preferred technology on the basis of firm, programmable output (unlike wind and solar), a proven UK and European deployment track record, a delivery timeline of 4–6 years from financial close, strong bankability under project finance structures, and a feedstock economic model that generates dual revenue — gate fees from waste acceptance and electricity revenue from the PPA — materially improving debt service coverage.

The dual-revenue model — gate fees from waste intake plus electricity revenue from the PPA — functions as a natural hedge. Gate fee income is largely independent of power price volatility, improving debt service coverage ratios and reducing the PPA pricing required to achieve bankable returns.

03 — Commercial Structure

A DBF(O)T model transferring all development and financing risk to the developer

The project was structured as a Design-Build-Finance-(Operate)-Transfer arrangement. FTP bears all development, permitting, EPC, and financing risk. A 20-year take-or-pay Power Purchase Agreement at terms competitive to the retail power price forms the contractual spine of the financing model, targeting senior debt at approximately 70% gearing.

Gate fees from waste intake — modelled at a blended average of approximately £108/tonne across 300–350ktpa — provide the second major revenue stream. The Freeport private-wire model provides material cost advantages: exemption from TNUoS and DUoS charges, exemption from BSUoS balancing charges, and Ofgem licensing simplification. These translate directly into a lower delivered cost per unit compared with grid-imported alternatives.

The private-wire Freeport model removes the three largest cost components of conventional grid supply — TNUoS, DUoS, and BSUoS — allowing the ERF to deliver power to port tenants at materially below-market rates while still achieving bankable project returns.

04 — Feedstock Strategy

A three-stream feedstock model targeting 300–350ktpa across Highland & Islands sources

A three-stream feedstock model was developed targeting an aggregate of 300–350ktpa. Primary supply was identified from Highland Council municipal solid waste residual streams, with secondary supply from commercial and industrial waste via the Longman transfer station and equivalent facilities across the H&I region.

Grade B and C waste wood from construction activity and forestry processing in the Highlands formed a third stream, aligned with Scottish Government residual waste policy and the requirement for R1 energy recovery classification under the Industrial Emissions Directive. Barge logistics from southern Highland Council collection points were modelled as the primary transport route, leveraging Port Nigg's existing quayside infrastructure.

05 — Regulatory & Permitting Pathway

Four concurrent consenting tracks with a CCS integration route identified

The permitting architecture was mapped across four concurrent tracks: SEPA integrated environmental permit under the IED, Highland Council planning permission (with EIA scope), Marine Scotland marine licence for barge operations, and Ofgem private-wire notification.

A Carbon Capture and Storage integration route was identified as a future value-accretive pathway, aligned with the North Sea Transition Authority's interest in CCS-ready industrial installations in the Cromarty Firth corridor. Exploratory engagement with The Highland Council confirmed constructive appetite to support the project at an early-stage pre-planning level.

07 — Outcome & Reflection

Exogenous timing factors, not proposition failure

The project did not proceed to the next development stage. The site owner confirmed that decommissioning of the Nigg Oil Terminal — the intended ERF location — had overrun its contractual completion date, making near-term site exclusivity unavailable. Alongside this, uncertainty about the tenant demand mix at the port during the critical 2026–2029 window made the 25MW take-or-pay structure difficult for the counterparty to commit to. These were exogenous timing factors, not failures of the development proposition itself.

Institutional capability built

In infrastructure origination, the discipline of building a bankable proposition — stress-testing technology assumptions, financing structures, and regulatory pathways against real counterparty engagement — creates durable institutional knowledge. The Port Nigg programme established FTP's capability across the full development lifecycle of an ERF project, from concept to commercial-ready proposition, in a live market environment.

Transferable insights
01
Site exclusivity timing is the critical gating factor in private-wire ERF financing — the development programme must be anchored to a confirmed site access timeline before commercial structuring can proceed to bankable terms.
02
Long-term take-or-pay tenors are structurally mismatched with the planning horizons of industrial port operators managing evolving tenant mixes — flexibility provisions and phased capacity commitments improve counterparty willingness to engage.
03
Dual-revenue-stream projects — combining gate fees with electricity offtake — materially improve debt service coverage ratios and reduce the required PPA price, creating a structurally more financeable proposition than single-revenue ERF models.
